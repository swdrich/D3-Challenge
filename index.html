Correlations Discovered Between Health Risks and Age, Income

As we cycle through the risk factors on the left-hand axis and compare them to the number of people at or below the poverty rate per state, we see positive correlations between poverty and a lack of access to health care, smoking rates, and obesity rates. While this chart does not have enough data to indicate a causal relationship, it is not impossible to imagine that poverty could play a key role in all of these factors. Poverty can limit the ability to access affordable healthcare, either because premiums are too high, or people don't have transportation or funds to manage chronic conditions effectively. It can also contribute to obesity by limiting people's access to healthy options, retail grocery stores with fresh produce, or a lack of time and/or facilities to cook health meals. Lastly, people in poverty are more likely to be smokers than the general public, and may not have access to the support or medications they need to help them quit.

Examining these three factors against each state's median population age, we do not see strong correlations between age and either healthcare access or obesity. There may be a slight trend for older people to have less healthcare, and a slight trend for older people also to be more obese, but neither appear on the surface to be statistically significant. When we examine the population of smokers, however, we see a strong correlation between older Americans being more likely to smoke than younger ones. This may be the payoff of several decades of strong anti-smoking public health campaigning directed at children, as well as younger adults who did smoke being more likely to be able to quit successfully.

Finally, looking at the risk factors versus median household income, we see many of the same trends that we saw in the analysis versus state poverty rates. As household income goes up, rates of healthcare access go up, and obesity and smoking rates drop significantly. In a country where most health insurance is linked to workers' jobs, it is unsurprising that as median income rises, presumably largely from wages, a larger percent of the population would also be insured. Likewise, having better access to health care could lead to better health overall, including lower incidences of obesity and more access to the resources and messaging that would help people quit smoking, or not start smoking to begin with.
